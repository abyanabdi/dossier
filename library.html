CORE INVESTIGATION SKILLS

Investigation Toolkit

SKILL MODULE 001

Email Analysis

Investigators examine digital communications to determine whether messages are authentic, misleading, or connected to a social engineering attack.

Investigators Examine:
  • Sender addresses and display names
  • Email domains and reply-to addresses
  • Urgency and pressure tactics
  • Suspicious links and attachments
  • Requests for personal or financial information

SKILL MODULE 002

Website Verification

Investigators verify websites and online organizations by examining domain information, security indicators, company records, and digital history.

Investigators Examine:
  • Domain registration information
  • Website creation dates
  • TLS and security certificates
  • Company contact information
  • Copied or inconsistent website content

Digital Footprint Analysis

Online accounts, public records, websites, and communication patterns can help investigators verify identities and uncover hidden connections.

Investigators Examine:
  • Social media profiles
  • Employment and company records
  • Account creation patterns
  • Online aliases
  • Connections between digital identities

SKILL MODULE 004

Audio Authentication

Investigators examine recordings for signs of editing, synthetic speech, voice cloning, and other forms of audio manipulation.

Investigators Examine:
  • Speech rhythm and pronunciation
  • Background noise inconsistencies
  • Frequency and waveform changes
  • Synthetic voice indicators

Identity Verification

THREAT RECOGNITION

Cybercrime Threat Database

THREAT LEVEL: HIGH

Social Engineering

Social engineering attacks manipulate trust, fear, urgency, authority, or curiosity to influence a victim's decisions.

Common Methods:
  • Fake employment opportunities
  • Authority impersonation
  • Urgent payment requests
  • Account verification scams
  • Requests for confidential information

THREAT LEVEL: HIGH

Phishing Attacks

Phishing messages are designed to steal information, deliver malicious files, or direct victims to fraudulent websites.

Common Warning Signs:
  • Suspicious sender addresses
  • Unexpected links or attachments
  • Spelling and formatting errors
  • Urgent action requests
  • Unusual login or payment pages

THREAT LEVEL: EMERGING

AI-Assisted Fraud

Artificial intelligence can be used to create realistic voices, images, messages, and identities that make fraudulent communications more convincing.

Investigators Analyze:
  • Voice cloning
  • Synthetic images
  • Manipulated video
  • Automated impersonation
  • AI-generated communications

THREAT LEVEL: HIGH

Business Email Compromise

Criminals impersonate executives, suppliers, or trusted employees to redirect payments or obtain sensitive information.

Common Indicators:
  • Unexpected payment changes
  • New banking instructions
  • Requests to avoid normal procedures
  • Executive impersonation
  • Pressure to act secretly

THREAT LEVEL: HIGH

Credential Theft

Credential theft occurs when attackers obtain passwords, authentication codes, or account access information.

Common Methods:
  • Fake login pages
  • Password reset scams
  • Malicious attachments
  • Session hijacking
  • Multi-factor authentication manipulation

THREAT LEVEL: CRITICAL

Ransomware

Ransomware attacks encrypt files or disrupt systems before demanding payment for restored access.

Investigators Examine:
  • Initial access methods
  • Malicious files and links
  • System activity logs
  • Encryption behavior
  • Data theft indicators

FORENSIC PROCEDURES

Digital and Physical Evidence Methods

Evidence Collection

Investigators collect information while protecting it from contamination, alteration, deletion, or unauthorized access.

Examples: Emails, files, screenshots, devices, recordings, documents, chemical samples, and account records.

Chain of Custody

Chain of custody is the documented history of how evidence was collected, transferred, stored, examined, and protected.

A complete record helps demonstrate that the evidence remained reliable throughout the investigation.

Metadata Analysis

Metadata can reveal when a file was created, edited, accessed, transferred, or recorded.

Investigators compare metadata with statements and timelines to identify inconsistencies.

Timeline Reconstruction

Investigators organize events in chronological order using communications, access logs, witness accounts, and forensic findings.

A reliable timeline can reveal opportunity, planning, movement, and contradictions.

Source Verification

Information should be confirmed using independent and reliable sources before it is accepted as evidence.

One message, profile, or statement is rarely enough to establish authenticity.

Evidence Corroboration

Corroboration occurs when separate pieces of evidence support the same conclusion.

Strong investigations rely on patterns across multiple sources rather than a single assumption.

Investigator Decision Process

Professional investigations require a structured approach:

Preserve Evidence → Verify Sources → Analyze Patterns → Compare Statements → Test Explanations → Document Findings

Investigators should avoid forming conclusions before all available evidence has been reviewed.

Investigator Checklist

Before closing an investigation, ask:

  • Was the evidence collected and preserved correctly?
  • Can the source of each item be verified?
  • Does the information match across multiple sources?
  • Are there signs of manipulation, deception, or contamination?
  • Which conclusions are directly supported by evidence?
  • Which conclusions are assumptions?
  • Are there contradictions that still need to be resolved?
  • Could another explanation fit the evidence?
  • How could the incident have been prevented?
